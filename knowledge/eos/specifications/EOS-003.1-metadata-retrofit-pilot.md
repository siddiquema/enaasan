# EOS-003.1: Metadata Retrofit Pilot — Findings

**Status:** Complete (findings for review)
**Version:** 1.0
**Last Updated:** 2026-07-13
**Owner:** Chief Knowledge Architect
**Applies:** EOS-003 v1.0 to the five Communication Skills cards (`publications/skills/communication/`)
**Purpose:** Empirical evidence about the contract's authoring cost and friction, gathered *before* applying it to the rest of the knowledge base. Refinements R1–R8 below SHOULD be applied to the metadata standard before any mass retrofit.

---

## 1. WHAT WAS DONE

The five communication cards (ENA-KCARD-SKILL-*) were retrofitted from their informal headers to the full EOS-003 contract — all Tier 1 + Tier 2 fields as YAML front matter — applying the interlock rules (V1–V10) honestly rather than generously.

**Headline finding first: the contract caught real dishonesty on its first contact with real content.**

- Two cards claimed **"Primary Research"** in their old headers. Under the weakest-load-bearing-claim rule (D4), Card 2 rated `expert-opinion` (its five-competency grouping is editorial synthesis) and Card 5 rated `anecdotal` (its A/B comparisons are constructed composites, and they ARE the card).
- Card 5's title claimed **"Real Examples."** They are illustrative composites. The title now says "Worked Examples." The old title was a small lie the philosophy's honesty commitment caught mechanically.
- Writing `description` against Card 4 surfaced a factual discrepancy (the series README calls the checklist "50-item"; it has 30 items).

This is the strongest possible validation of the contract's premise: metadata that traces to commitments *finds problems*, it doesn't just describe content.

## 2. TIME TO AUTHOR

| Card | Judgment time (est.) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Card 1 (definition) | ~15 min | Slowest — first contact; every field pattern had to be invented |
| Card 2 (framework) | ~10 min | Evidence demotion decision took most of it |
| Card 3 (mental model) | ~12 min | Hardest evidence call (illustration vs. data, §3.2) |
| Card 4 (checklist) | ~8 min | Patterns established by now |
| Card 5 (example) | ~10 min | Evidence + title honesty decisions |
| **Total** | **~55 min for 5 cards** | Mechanical fields take seconds; ALL of the time is judgment on ~6 fields |

**Learning curve is real:** halved from first card to fourth. With the R1 authoring template below, steady-state estimate is **5–8 minutes per card** — acceptable. Without refinements, a naive author facing 24 undifferentiated fields would take 20+ minutes and resent most of them.

## 3. FIELDS THAT CAUSED HESITATION

**3.1 `stakes` — hesitated on 4 of 5 cards.** The low/medium boundary is genuinely fuzzy: a definition card is "low" (error costs a re-read), but the checklist card steers role choices — medium. What resolved it each time was re-reading the contract's worked definitions ("consequence of this being wrong"). Confirms the contract's own instinct: stakes needs a **reviewer confirmation step**, plus 2–3 more worked examples per level in the standard.

**3.2 `evidence` under the weakest-claim rule — the hardest calls (cards 2, 3, 5).** The 5-level scale has no value for **illustrative composites** — constructed scenarios built on real data. Resolution reached (and recommended as convention, not a 6th level):
- If the composite is load-bearing → rate `anecdotal`, state "illustrative composite" in `evidence_notes`, and require in-body labeling (Card 5)
- If the composite only explains a mechanism whose claim rests on real data elsewhere → the data's rating stands, composite disclosed in notes (Card 3)
The distinction that does the work: **is the constructed material load-bearing for the card's claim, or explanatory?**

**3.3 `uncertainty` on definitional content (card 1).** "What's uncertain about a definition?" stalled briefly. Answer found: *framing uncertainty* — "this is one widely-used framing, not a universal standard." Every category has a natural uncertainty type; authors need the cheat-sheet (R4), not the field removed.

**3.4 `outcome` capability-phrasing (V4).** Every card needed a deliberate rewrite to avoid decision-phrasing and land on "student can judge/evaluate/apply…". The rule works — and it noticeably improved the outcomes (each now names a *transferable* capability, which is the agency principle doing its job) — but it does not come naturally. Template needs 2–3 model sentences.

**3.5 `review_due` — exposed a rule gap.** Card 3 is `stakes: medium` (V2 says ≤12 months) but contains salary data that ages in months. Set to 6 months by judgment. **Finding: review_due is a function of stakes × volatility, and volatility is currently nowhere in the contract.** See R2.

**3.6 `purpose` vs `description` overlap.** For atomic cards these converge. Resolved with a pattern: *description = what this is; purpose = the decision or fear it serves*. Needs one line in the standard (R7).

## 4. FIELDS THAT COULD BE INFERRED AUTOMATICALLY

Eleven of ~24 fields required zero judgment and could be supplied by tooling or defaults:

| Field | Inference source |
|---|---|
| `id` | folder + filename convention |
| `type`, `category` | template used |
| `domain` | folder (domain-first layout, A3) |
| `language` | content |
| `created`, `updated` | git history |
| `version`, `status` | workflow defaults (0.x + draft) |
| `license` | default constant |
| `visibility` | derivable from status (see §6) |
| `register` | category default (letter/student-voice → voice; else reference) |

**Implication:** the *authored* surface of the contract is ~8–10 fields, not 24. The standard should say so loudly (R1) — the contract's perceived weight is a documentation problem, not a design problem.

---

## 5. FIELDS BETTER OWNED BY REVIEWERS THAN AUTHORS

1. **`stakes`** — contract already says so (2.4); the pilot confirms it. Author proposes, reviewer confirms.
2. **`evidence`** — *new finding:* authors over-claim. Both pre-pilot "Primary Research" labels were author-assigned and wrong. Author proposes with `evidence_notes`; reviewer applies the weakest-claim rule adversarially.
3. **`maturity`** — already capped (authors ≤ `developing`); pilot default of `seed` felt right and cost nothing.
4. **`review_due`** — should be owned by *neither*: computed from the stakes × volatility table (R2). Every hand-set date is a future inconsistency.

---

## 6. METADATA THAT FELT BUREAUCRATIC RATHER THAN USEFUL

Honest list, with dispositions:

- **`visibility`** — on every card it was mechanically `internal` because status was `draft`. At current scale it is 100% derivable from status. **Disposition:** keep the concept (do-no-harm needs it once exceptions exist — e.g., external reviewer access), but make it a *derived* field until a real exception forces explicit values (R5).
- **`updated`** — hand-maintained duplicate of what git knows; it WILL drift. **Disposition:** derive from git in the representation layer (R6).
- **`relationships: []`** — an empty required list on Card 1 is noise. **Disposition:** omit-when-empty; "one stored direction only" already means many objects legitimately store nothing.
- **`tags`** — added minimal tags in ~5 seconds without conviction. Exactly as the contract predicted (1.15: "most marginal field"). **Disposition:** keep as-is (optional, non-structural); revisit only if anything ever consumes them.
- **NOT bureaucratic, despite being the most work:** `purpose`, `outcome`, `evidence`, `uncertainty`, `stakes`. These five consumed ~80% of authoring time and generated 100% of the value (every §1 catch came from them). The cost sits exactly where the philosophy says it should.

## 7. REFINEMENTS TO APPLY BEFORE MASS RETROFIT (R1–R8)

| # | Refinement | Addresses |
|---|---|---|
| R1 | **Authoring template with a hand-written core of ~8 fields** (title, description, purpose, outcome, stage, audience, evidence+notes, uncertainty; stakes as proposal); all else defaulted/inferred, clearly marked | §2, §4 |
| R2 | **`review_due` computed from a stakes × volatility rule table**; volatility signaled by a lightweight flag (`volatile: true` for salary/exam/fee/cutoff data) or inferred from content type | §3.5 |
| R3 | **Illustrative-composite convention** documented in the standard: load-bearing composite → `anecdotal` + notes label + in-body labeling; explanatory composite → underlying data's rating + disclosure | §3.2 |
| R4 | **Per-category `uncertainty` cheat-sheet** (definition → framing variance; data → verify-live instructions; checklist → self-assessment limits; example → composite disclaimer) | §3.3 |
| R5 | **`visibility` derived from status** until a real exception appears | §6 |
| R6 | **`updated` sourced from git**, not hand-maintained | §6 |
| R7 | **One-line disambiguation:** description = what it is; purpose = the decision or fear it serves. Plus 3 model capability-phrased outcomes | §3.4, §3.6 |
| R8 | **Review gate:** reviewer explicitly confirms `stakes` and `evidence` (adversarially, weakest-claim), and is the only party who may raise `maturity` | §5 |

**Sequencing note:** applying R1–R8 to `metadata-standard.md` *is* the refined form of amendment A4 — executed once, informed by evidence, instead of blindly. That was the point of piloting first.

---

## 8. VERDICT

The contract is **writable by humans in reasonable time, and it earns its cost** — it caught two inflated evidence claims, one dishonest title, and one factual discrepancy across just five cards that had already been written carefully. The friction found is concentrated, explainable, and fixable by R1–R8 without touching the contract's structure. No field needs to be removed; two need to become derived; the five expensive fields are expensive for the right reasons.

**Recommended next steps:** approve R1–R8 → apply them to `metadata-standard.md` (refined A4) → proceed to EOS-004 (Relationship Model), whose vocabulary the retrofitted cards are already exercising (`requires`, `exemplifies`).
