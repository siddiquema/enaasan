# How to Get Your First Job

## Introduction

Most advice about first jobs focuses on what you need to do: polish your resume, practice interviews, network, learn new skills.

All true. But it skips the part that actually changes how you prepare.

Here's a different starting point: **Stop trying to guess what employers want. Start thinking like one.**

This isn't about passing a test. It's about understanding the perspective on the other side of the interview.

---

## The Reflection

**Imagine you own a small company and need to hire one graduate.**

After reviewing many applications and conducting interviews, you have shortlisted two candidates.

Both have similar qualifications.

Both have similar academic scores.

Both meet the basic requirements for the role.

Now you need to decide whom to hire.

During the interview, the first candidate listens carefully, explains their ideas clearly, asks relevant questions, and is honest about what they know and what they still need to learn.

The second candidate has similar qualifications but struggles to explain their thoughts, gives very brief answers, and doesn't show much curiosity about the role or the organization.

If you could choose only one candidate, who would you hire?

Most people choose the first candidate.

The reason is simple. They appear more prepared to work with others, learn new things, and contribute to the organization.

This doesn't mean that academic performance is unimportant. Your education helps you build knowledge and opens the door to opportunities.

However, once that door opens, employers also look for qualities that are difficult to measure through marks alone.

They want to understand how you communicate, how you approach problems, how willing you are to learn, and how you might work with a team.

### Pause and Think

**If you were hiring for your own organization, what three qualities would you look for in a new graduate?**

Now ask yourself:

**Which of those qualities do I already have, and which ones should I start developing?**

---

## What This Teaches You

The qualities you just wrote down? Those are not what employers see on your resume.

They're not looking at your GPA or your degree or how many languages you speak. Those things matter, but they're background noise compared to what you just identified.

Employers are evaluating whether you're someone they can actually work with. Whether you'll listen when they point out a mistake. Whether you'll show up reliably. Whether you'll ask for help when you're confused. Whether you'll communicate what you're doing instead of disappearing for three days.

You probably already know all this about yourself.

The work isn't learning these things. It's being honest about where you stand, and focusing on the things that actually matter in the first 90 days of a job.

---

## That's It

This guide is short because the insight does all the work.

You've now done what most candidates never do: you've stopped thinking like someone trying to get hired, and started thinking like someone who has to hire. That shift changes everything about how you prepare.

Your resume, your interview answers, what you practice — it all flows from this perspective once you have it.

Your first job isn't about proving you're smart enough. It's about showing you're someone worth developing.

You already know what that means. Trust that.
