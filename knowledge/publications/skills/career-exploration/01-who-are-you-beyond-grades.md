# Who Are You? Beyond Grades

### One Question Before You Apply for Your Next Job

Most graduates ask:

- Which job pays more?
- Which company is hiring?
- Which certification should I do?
- Which course should I learn?

Very few ask:

> What kind of work will I enjoy doing for the next five or ten years?

Getting a job is important. Getting into the wrong kind of job is expensive. You may spend years doing work that drains you simply because nobody asked you to understand yourself before choosing a career.

Your degree tells employers what you studied.

Your grades tell employers how well you performed academically.

Neither tells you whether you will enjoy or succeed in a particular career.

Before exploring careers, spend a few minutes understanding yourself.

---

## The Four Things That Matter

When thinking about your career, there are four things that matter more than your grades.

1. Your strengths.
2. Your interests.
3. Your values.
4. Your preferred working style.

You don't need perfect answers. You only need honest answers.

---

## 1. Your Strengths

Strengths are not subjects that you scored well in.

A strength is something that:

- Comes naturally to you.
- Improves quickly with practice.
- Other people notice and appreciate.
- Gives you energy instead of draining you.

### Ask Yourself

- What do people usually ask me for help with?
- What kind of tasks do I enjoy doing without being asked?
- What role do I naturally play during group activities?
- What work makes me feel satisfied when I finish it?

### Common Strengths

- Teaching and explaining concepts
- Building relationships
- Organising work and systems
- Solving problems
- Creative thinking
- Strategic thinking
- Attention to detail
- Research and analysis
- Execution and delivery
- Leadership and coordination

---

## 2. Your Interests

Interests are things that naturally attract your attention.

Ask yourself:

- What topics do I enjoy reading or watching videos about?
- What industries or professions make me curious?
- If nobody judged me, what would I spend more time learning?
- Which conversations excite me the most?

### Examples

You may enjoy learning about:

- Technology
- Business
- Finance
- Teaching and learning
- Psychology and human behaviour
- Health and wellbeing
- Design and creativity
- Data and analytics
- Organisations and leadership
- Entrepreneurship

There are no good or bad answers.

---

## 3. Your Values

Values are the things that matter most to you when making career decisions.

Different people value different things.

Some common examples include:

- Financial security
- Learning and growth
- Work-life balance
- Creativity
- Recognition and career growth
- Helping others
- Autonomy and independence
- Stability and predictability
- Excellence and mastery

### Ask Yourself

If two jobs paid exactly the same salary, which would you choose?

- The one that allows you to learn more?
- The one that gives you more freedom?
- The one that offers better stability?
- The one that creates greater impact?

Your answer tells you something important about yourself.

---

## 4. Your Working Style

Many people dislike their jobs not because of the work itself, but because they dislike the way the work is done.

Think about how you prefer to work.

### People

- Do you enjoy talking to people all day?
- Do you prefer independent work?

### Environment

- Do you like structure and clear processes?
- Do you prefer flexibility and experimentation?

### Pace

- Do you enjoy working on many things quickly?
- Do you prefer spending time going deep into one problem?

### Nature of Work

- Do you enjoy building things?
- Do you enjoy analysing things?
- Do you enjoy teaching things?
- Do you enjoy managing people?

## Your Career Snapshot

Take five minutes and write down your answers.

### Strengths

Pick your top three.

### Interests

Pick your top three.

### Values

Pick your top three.

### Working Style

Describe your ideal work environment in three or four sentences.

## Important

You are not choosing your career for life.

You are only trying to make your next career decision more intelligently.

Most people discover their strengths through experience. Your answers today may change over time.

Think of this exercise as creating a career snapshot of who you are today.

---

## Outcome

After completing this guide, you should be able to:

- Understand yourself beyond grades and degrees.
- Describe your strengths and interests clearly.
- Identify the kind of work environment that suits you.
- Explore careers that are aligned with who you are today.
- Make better decisions about jobs, courses and certifications.
