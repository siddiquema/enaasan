After 10th — Choosing Your Stream

Choose based on a combination of interest, strength, and the kind of future you're picturing — not the stream's reputation. Science suits students aiming at engineering, medicine, tech, or research. Commerce suits students interested in accounts, business, finance, CA, or BBA-type paths. Arts/Humanities suits students drawn to languages, social sciences, law, psychology, design, or teaching. All three lead to strong careers — the "hierarchy" between them is outdated perception, not current reality.

If your JEE rank is lower than hoped, CUET-based B.Tech seats at several central universities, or strong private colleges with good lab and project culture, are reasonable alternatives — in many fields, the skills you build matter more than the college brand on your degree. (Note: DU's own B.Tech intake runs on JEE Main, not CUET.)

It depends on how close you already are to your target rank, whether you have a clear and structured preparation plan, and whether you have the stress tolerance and family support to sustain another year of intense prep. If you're genuinely close with a plan, it can be worth it. If it's driven mainly by not knowing what else to do, joining a good college and preparing in parallel is usually the better path.

After 12th — Commerce Stream

Commerce opens a genuinely wide range of paths: BCom, BBA, Chartered Accountancy (CA), Company Secretary (CS), Cost and Management Accountancy (CMA), Economics, Business Analytics, Digital Marketing, Finance, and — if you're comfortable with maths — Data Science. It's also a strong base for entrepreneurship.

CA is high-effort with a high payoff if you clear it — it's a demanding, multi-year qualification, but it opens strong, specialised career doors. BCom followed by an MBA or CFA is generally the "safer" and less pressured path, with strong outcomes but a longer, more gradual build. The right choice depends on your risk tolerance and how confident you are in sustained, exam-heavy preparation.

After 12th — Arts & Humanities

Arts and Humanities lead to law, psychology, sociology, economics, journalism and media, design, teaching, public administration, social work, and hospitality — along with newer fields like UX, content strategy, and digital marketing. A few notes on specific paths: Psychology suits people interested in counselling, HR, or research roles. Law is competitive to break into but leads to strong, stable careers once established. Design is heavily skill- and portfolio-based, so what you build matters more than the credential alone.

If you're in engineering and want deep technical specialisation, M.Tech or an MS is worth considering. Category 6 · 7 questions

The Bigger Picture

Start with three questions, not a career test: What subjects do you enjoy even when there's no exam pressure? What kind of problems do you like solving — people problems, technical problems, or creative problems? And what does a "good day at work" look like to you? Your answers point toward broad directions long before you need to pick an exact job title.

Very few career choices are permanent or unfixable. Most people change direction at least once, and skills like communication, problem-solving, and learning ability transfer across fields. The bigger risk isn't picking "wrong" — it's picking based on pressure or comparison rather than your own reasoning, since that makes it harder to course-correct later.

Treat this as a false choice. A better frame: pick a field with reasonable job demand where you can build genuine competence, and look for your passion inside how you do that work, not just in the job title. Passion often grows once you get good at something — it doesn't always come first.

A simple test: imagine no one would ever ask you about this choice again after today. Would you still pick it? AI-resilient fields tend to include healthcare, law, education, specialised engineering, cybersecurity, data science paired with real domain expertise, creative roles, and management. The pattern across all of them is combining solid domain knowledge with genuine AI fluency — using the tools well, rather than trying to out-compete them.

Increasingly, certifications and portfolios carry real weight alongside a degree, not instead of one in most cases. Recognised certifications (Google, AWS, Coursera and similar), a visible GitHub or project portfolio, and internships or freelance work are things many employers now treat as genuine, demonstrable proof of skill — often more convincing than a transcript alone.

These are real, growing options, and increasingly employers value skills over formal degrees in these spaces. Build a portfolio, pick up relevant certifications, and take on real projects. Starting with internships or freelance work is a low-risk way to test whether a non-traditional path genuinely fits before committing to it fully.

Category 7 · 2 questions

For Parents

Ask more than you advise, at least at first — "What do you enjoy about this?" tells you more than stating your own opinion. Share your concerns honestly, but frame them as one input among several rather than the final word. Decisions that are truly co-owned by the student tend to be pursued with more commitment than ones handed down.

Worry less about the field label and more about whether there's a realistic plan — income potential, skill-building path, and a fallback if plan A doesn't work out. Non-traditional fields have matured significantly and now have visible career ladders; the right question is "what's the plan," not "is this a real career."
